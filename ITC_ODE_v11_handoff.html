Organization Design & Execution

Organization Design and Execution is about making sure the structure matches the work. The focus is on three things: making clear who owns which decisions and which work, removing the bottlenecks that slow capable teams down, and getting the operation ready for the workforce and technology changes already underway.

Most clients start with a conversation about what they already know is not working and what they want to be different. From there, the work becomes a project. We move at your pace. What we design, your team owns and runs. Most organizations at this scale see measurable value inside ninety days, in fewer escalations, faster decision cycles on critical work, and labor efficiency the CFO can see on the P&L.

What we help with
Who Owns the Work
Decisions keep stalling. The same calls land on the same two people every time.

Critical decisions bounce upward and wait. Everyone is busy. Nothing is moving. It’s not that the team can’t decide — it’s that nobody has ever gotten clear on who actually owns which decisions and which work.

How we work through it
  • Map where decisions consistently stall or land with the wrong person
  • Design decision flow around the speed the operation actually needs
What changes for you
  • Decisions made at the right level without constant escalation
  • Accountability that holds when leaders or teams change

For leadership: every decision that escalates unnecessarily costs your team time and costs you attention. This builds the structure that stops it.

Execution That Holds
Initiatives start strong. They slow in the middle and stall before they land.

Projects stall not because the team can’t deliver, but because nobody knows who unblocks it when it gets stuck. The gap between what was designed and what actually runs is almost always structural — not a people problem.

How we work through it
  • Identify where execution breaks down and the structural cause behind each point
  • Redesign operating conditions so work flows without constant senior intervention
What changes for you
  • Visible improvement in execution speed within 90 days
  • Fewer initiatives abandoned mid-stream for structural reasons

For leadership: every initiative that stalls in the middle is a budget line that didn’t deliver. This closes the gap between what was approved and what actually ran.

AI Accountability
The AI is already in. Nobody defined who owns what happens when it’s wrong.

Tools are deployed and processes changed. But nobody defined who owns the exceptions, the oversight, or what happens when the output is wrong. That gap is not a technology problem. It’s an accountability problem.

How we work through it
  • Map which roles have changed because of AI tools already in use or being implemented
  • Design the accountability structure for human-AI workflows before a failure forces the question
What changes for you
  • Clarity on who is responsible when an AI-assisted process needs review or reversal
  • AI adoption that holds because people understand their place in it

For leadership: AI tool failures surface as operational incidents. This builds the ownership structure before the first one lands.

From the field
Situation

A $140M division inside a Fortune 500 foodservice equipment company had missed its numbers three quarters in a row. Corporate needed visible progress before the next board cycle but could not impose a top-down restructuring — the semi-autonomous model was a core part of how the company operated across all twenty-three of its divisions.

Challenge

The division’s leadership team had strong tenure but had never operated under a structured performance management cadence. Prior corporate programs had been launched and quietly abandoned, and the workforce had learned to wait them out. Nobody had ever built a governance structure the division actually owned — so when things went sideways, there was nowhere for accountability to land.

Outcomes and Signs of Winning

EBITDA moved from 2% to 14%. Every leader on the division team stayed through close. Two of the other fourteen operating companies asked to use the same governance model without being asked. The board stopped wondering whether the people running the business had a handle on it.

Situation

A PE-backed commercial kitchen and HVACR service platform had been assembled through three acquisitions over four years, reaching $160M in revenue across fourteen markets. Each acquired business had kept its own dispatch system, its own technician standards, and its own pricing model. The sponsor had a platform thesis. The platform did not function as one.

Challenge

Field productivity varied 40% between the highest- and lowest-performing markets. Five markets were running below customer satisfaction thresholds that triggered contract renewal risk. The COO had clear financial targets for the next twelve months and no operational baseline reliable enough to build a plan from. The structure looked like a platform. It ran like a collection of separate businesses.

Outcomes and Signs of Winning

Field productivity improved 32% across all fourteen markets within twelve months. Five markets crossed above customer satisfaction renewal thresholds. Overtime cost dropped 18% from dispatch efficiency gains. The platform had a documented operating model it could replicate into future acquisitions.

Situation

A mid-market manufacturer had installed automation across two production facilities with clear productivity targets in the investment case. Eighteen months after installation, the targets had not been met. Turnover in the automated facilities was running at 34% annually — higher than the manual plants still running the old way. The CFO was asking hard questions. The technology vendor had finished their scope and left.

Challenge

Operators did not understand the new systems. First-line supervisors were reverting to manual overrides to prevent downtime, which was undermining the entire automation investment. Nobody had defined who owned the human side of what the technology now required — the exceptions, the judgment calls, the oversight. The structure had been redesigned around the equipment. It had never been redesigned around the people running it.

Outcomes and Signs of Winning

Turnover in the automated facilities dropped from 34% to 19% within nine months. Supervisor manual override incidents decreased 61%. The productivity metrics from the original investment case were achieved in month eleven — thirteen months after they were first projected. The COO had a documented workforce model ready to apply to the two remaining manual facilities.
